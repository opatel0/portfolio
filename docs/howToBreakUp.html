How to Break Up with a Bird

 “For you, dear Verna, reaches my poor heart,

but only clutches air where you once perched;

the arrow’s point would likely have done its part

if only you were there to feel its burn.

The stone by the lake where looked for trout

did fail you so you flew away too soon;

my love cannot feed you like the lake’s trout,

so once again I’m left alone to swoon.

But soon you’ll come again to look for food;

you’ll find a letter folded there instead.

A message you may find abrupt or crude,

will open itself and aloud will read:

‘You’re the most beautiful creature I’ve ever seen.

Verna, oh Verna, will you marry me?’”

Peter jumped off the stool and collapsed onto his solar system rug. He stayed there, a tangled mess on the ground, listening to the deep silence outside. The lake house was the only property around for another two miles down the road so he was used to the quiet, but the fog muffled even the sounds of the lake.

Leaving his poem lying on the floor by Saturn, Peter stood up, walked over to his desk, and took a seat, scouting for Verna through his bedroom window. It was a nervous tick, the way he took quick glances outside all day. Growing antsy after a few moments, he called, “Verna!” Only a chill whispered through his window, taunting him like a secret. If Verna were out there, the fog would never tell.

*

Peter’s fingers traveled to the mug sitting at the corner of his desk. His late grandfather’s old watch hung on its brim and a tall, blue feather stood inside. The muddled light from the window made the two reflect off each other, emanating a bluish gold. The watch had never worked, even when it belonged to his grandfather. As far as Peter knew, it had been handcrafted to always read 8:47. His grandfather used to say, “What good is the time anyway? Time, watches, they’re ornaments, a way of dressing the world up in little tutus.” Peter put the watch on and ran his finger along its face as a way of polishing it. It’s all Peter had left to remember him by. When his parents decided to move into his grandfather’s old lake house, he thought he could at least have that, but his parents completely transformed it: renovated it, refurnished it, even added to it. Because they could afford to buy their happiness, they added a sunroom in the back. Peter determined never to step foot in it. You might as well be desecrating his tombstone.

As Peter meditated on that, his hand returned to the mug absentmindedly. His small frame huddled over his desk as he smoothed the feather’s blue barbs until they ran evenly, as if to say, “Verna, give me a chance.”

It was 4:30 p.m. Peter still had the house to himself for a half hour before Barry, his dad’s college buddy from Salisbury, arrived. Peter met him a few years before at a restaurant in Baltimore. Even though it was a nice restaurant, Barry wore oil stains all over his blue three-button collar shirt and cargo shorts. Even the cloth napkins looked nicer than him.

Barry and his dad got on well enough. His dad kept calling him “you damn cheat,” though it sounded more like a compliment. Peter just couldn’t understand him, couldn’t figure out his eyes. They flicked around the restaurant constantly, as if he were waiting for the right moment to rob the place blind, as if in a few moments, he’d hold everyone in the place hostage.

And those shifty eyes were on their way to Peter’s little lake house in South Carolina. ETA: 5 p.m. Peter’s dad wouldn’t be home from work until 7:00, and his mom not until 11:30. Two hours alone with the cheat.

“Why do you have to work?” Peter asked.

“I won’t have this conversation, Peter.” His mom was doing her final check before heading out the door, wearing the cold expression of an anesthesiologist. She was in the kitchen, though it was practically the same room as the living room where Peter sat; the only thing that separated them was a long stretch of counter.

“Why doesn’t he just come after 7?”

“Don’t be that way.” She wiped the already clean counter down and hung the dish towel over the faucet. “This means a lot to your father, so please just behave.”

“I’m just asking. It doesn’t make sense.”

“That’s enough, Peter.” She picked up her purse and headed to the door.

“But why did you have to—”

“Peter!”

They never explained things to Peter. He was lower level management, given only enough information to keep things tidy. The grand scheme is a commodity for parents, teachers, and the dentist, and they can all suck it. Right after thinking it, he ran to his frog backpack and pulled out his notepad to write it down. The front cover had a little Albert Einstein sticker on the corner and “FORGET THE REST” written diagonally across in thick, black marker. The notepad was almost full, mostly with notes about Verna.

Peter shoved the notepad into his back pocket. One day, he would recite his poems to Verna and on that same day, he swore he’d propose. There wouldn’t be a ceremony, a witness, or any of that. Just a proposal and maybe dinner. Verna could swallow a striped bass and Peter would down a few pretzels.

She stood her ground and retracted her neck into an “S” shape. She was standing on a big stone edging the lake and could look at him at eye level. “Petey, I’m way too old for you. You’re only thirteen.”

        “So in two weeks’ time, you decided that you want to have kids?”

        Verna extended her neck and looked up past Peter’s head. “Maybe you can spend more time trying to make friends. You know, with humans, and people your age.”

        “I don’t want new friends. They’d end up leaving me too.”

        Verna snapped her bill down at Peter. “Peter, don’t try to make me feel like that.”

        “It’s true. My grandfather was my only friend, he left me. Now you’re leaving me. Everyone’s going to leave me.”

        “Don’t be that way. You’re taking things way too far.”

        “I just don’t understand what I did. Why are you leaving me?” They stood in silence for a few moments. Then Peter muttered, “You’re not… I mean, you haven’t met someone, have you?”

Verna stood tall and flapped her wings violently, jerking her neck back and forth. “What? I can’t believe you would even! I would never—that’s—!” Her squawks sounded like grating metal.

        “Verna, don’t overreact, I just wanted to—”

        “No, Peter! You crossed the line, and I don’t even want to hear it.”

        She flew away before Peter could say anything more.

The front door banged three times. He’s twenty minutes early. Peter walked down the hall toward the living room, trying to ignore his parents’ wedding photos and the big collage of their vacation to Hawaii. There was nothing left of his grandfather except for his sepia-toned navy portrait with a wilting garland of daisies hung around the frame. Since he was buried all the way in Oregon, Peter’s parents bought a garland for his portrait every year on his death anniversary. Peter didn’t care for the tradition. It only took a day or two for the flowers to start smelling like dust.

Peter walked through his living room to the door and opened it. Behind the tattering net of the screen door stood a tan, almost brown man who stood at about 6’3”. Holy smokes.

“Is Barry here?” the man asked, raking his hand along his stubble.

Peter’s vision pulsed to readjust to his current situation. The man wore faded jeans and a loose yellow button up that hung low on his scrawny frame. The top three buttons were undone and his thick, gold chain glimmered against his silver chest tuft. Peter tried to look at his eyes to see if they were friendly, but the man’s sunglasses only reflected his own nervous expression.

“I’m a friend of his,” the man continued. “He told me to meet him here.” The man rubbed his hand along his buzzed, gray head. Peter never trusted a widow’s peak.

“You must have the wrong house.”

The man looked around, then eyed Peter carefully. “Could you direct me to another house in the area I could check?”

Peter fingered the fraying corner of the netting on the screen door. “There’s a house about a mile or two down the street.”

The man sighed. “Then this must be the house.” He stepped back from the door and stretched. “I’m only here to see him since he’s in the area.” He moved off the porch and carefully wrapped his bike lock around the bike frame and one of the porch posts. “Do you mind if I wait here for him?”

Peter didn’t say anything. This is not in the job description. This guy is not in the customer demographic. Mom, how the hell am I supposed to deal with this?

“I can wait out here,” he continued.

“You can sit on the porch swing,” Peter decided out loud. The porch swing was already rocking slightly, cutting its own space out in the fog.

“Alright.” The man moved to the swing. His footsteps resonated throughout the floor and up into Peter’s heart, racing his blood through his arteries like antelope. The man sat and swung his feet up onto the wood, resting his back against the swing chain. “Do me a favor, will you? I’m thirsty as hell and need a glass of water. Could you get one for me?”

Peter figured if he was going to accommodate a space for him, he might as well be a host. He walked into the kitchen and, while pouring water from the tap, watched the man through the window.

“And would you mind putting some ice in it?” he yelled through the window.

Peter brought the ice water out to him, and stood there next to him.

“Thanks, kid.” He moved his neck around like he was inspecting the place, satisfied, as evident by his slight grin.

“Why are you wearing sunglasses? It’s foggy out.”

The man raised his eyebrows. “They’re really just for decoration. Do you want me to take them off?”

Peter felt the weight of his watch on his wrist. “I don’t care.”

The man took four large gulps from his glass of water. It sounded like he was drowning.

“What’s your name, kid?” He set the glass on the ground next to him.

Shit.

“My name’s Rosie.” He rolled up his shirt sleeves and revealed a tattoo of a whale with a rose for a tail. The bulge of a long vein ran through the whale’s underbelly.

“Your real name’s Rosie?”

“No, it’s a nickname.” He took his sunglasses off and hung them on his necklace. “My real name is Chad.”

Peter leaned on the railing of the porch and it was from this angle he noticed a black backpack sitting by the door. It had a patch on the front that said, “Stand your ground.” He was going to ask about it, but also from this angle, he noticed Chad’s socks. He had on one argyle sock and one navy blue. “Why do you wear two different socks?”

Chad surveyed the house, glancing from the porch to the roof to the side yards. His eyes were moving like Barry’s, except they didn’t look to steal; they looked like they were taking pictures.

I should go back to my room and watch out for Verna. Peter didn’t move, just shifted his weight.

He could hear the faint sound of squealing tires approaching. That must be Barry. Of course his car is shit. The car came around the bend and Peter could make out an old Buick. It used to be a dark blue, it seemed, but the paint was so faded it was hard to tell. The car must have been running on its fifth wind. How could that thing still be going?

Barry parked on the street and grabbed a duffle bag from the passenger seat as he stepped out. He hung it from his shoulder and leaned with it as he walked. It looked like he was wearing the same outfit as last time Peter saw him, but two sizes larger. Man, he got big.

Walking back down the hall, Peter tried to imagine what the walls used to look like, feeling the absence of the old plaques and photos of their lineage: babies, reunions, and oh so many weddings. It gave the rickety hall the whole must of their ancestry.

Peter’s favorite item was the now missing centerpiece, the Vietnam Navy Gallantry Cross his grandfather was awarded for his service in Vietnam. It was a gold anchor on a thin gold chain, hanging from a red thumbtack on a small canvas wrapped in red velvet. Peter used to daydream about his grandfather passing it onto him, pulling the canvas right off the wall and putting it in his hands.

Hanging in its place was a portrait of his mother with her head resting on her fist. Her smile and acne-free face were airbrushed like the wall was. Peter hated this wall, more than he hated his parents for losing the anchor. He hated that his grandfather’s portrait hung at the far right side of the wall just before entering the living room, as if his grandfather’s memory was an afterthought.

“I heard of a girl the other day who fell down the drain.” Peter’s grandfather said. They were walking around the lake, the red leaves of the big maple struggling to hang on.

“Oh, I heard about that. She didn’t fall exactly.” Peter paused their pace to pick up a stone and threw it into the lake. The rings undulated undisturbed without a single animal, rock, or sand bar poised on the surface to disturb the rhythm. “She tethered herself to her shower head using a mountain climbing harness, but as she was descending, the shower head broke out of the wall and she went plummeting down. No one’s heard from her in weeks.”

“I have a plumber friend who has.” Peter’s grandfather took a few steps back and sat on a log he’d moved there years ago. “He was dismantling the pipes looking for the girl, when he came upon a note in her handwriting. It said, ‘Don’t worry, I’m safe. They’re taking good care of me.’ What do you suppose that means?”

Peter moved and squatted next to him. “Well, I’m sure it has something to do with the tiny people living in the sewers. There’s a legend about them luring children to them with playful music and sweets. The children follow them to a small palace they’ve made, with a throne and everything. The tiny people worship them like gods, bringing stolen foods and luxuries like sun hats and monocles.”

Peter’s grandfather eased himself into a sly grin. “Well, then I trust she’s in good hands.”

“I’ll have to visit them and ask.”

Peter’s grandfather let out a dry cackle. The lake had resettled and he lost himself in thought looking at it. “Look at the lake… how still it is. It looks like you could walk right through and be in another version of our world.”

“I wish I could walk right into it. I’d find my stuck-up parents and give them each a big smack.”

Peter’s grandfather laughed until he cried. “I got to tell you, Peter, I’ve never had so much fun in my life.”

Peter said nothing.

“You stay cool, alright?”

        Chad didn’t even get a passing glance as he walked to the front yard.

        Peter stood at the edge of the lake. She’s not coming. He took out his notepad and looked through, looking for a poem he would’ve read to her. He flipped through frantically, not even scanning them, deciding on nothing. Why am I waiting for you anyway? He closed the notepad and held it at his side. He thought about Barry inside, passed out on the bed. Fat-ass blowhard. He thought about his grandfather, Chad, and his poetry. He gave them all he could, but they didn’t budge for him. One was dead, one was an idiot, and one just sat wherever he put it like a paperweight, like sparse flecks of snow that disappear into the ground where they hit. What a waste. What good is it anyway? Peter threw the notepad on the ground and started for the house, but stopped after a few steps. He walked, almost ran back to the edge of the lake and the words burst out of him:

        “Fly to hell, babe! Forget the rest!”
